Economics is the study of how individuals, businesses, and governments make choices about allocating scarce resources. Because virtually everything you want — time, money, land, clean air, skilled labor — exists in limited supply, every choice involves a trade-off. When you decide to spend an evening studying instead of working, you are making an economic decision. When a government chooses to build a highway rather than fund a hospital, it is making one too. Economics gives you the frameworks and vocabulary to analyze these choices rigorously, predict their consequences, and evaluate whether they lead to desirable outcomes for individuals and for society as a whole.

## Microeconomics vs Macroeconomics

Economics divides naturally into two broad branches that operate at different scales.

**Microeconomics** focuses on the behavior of individual actors — a single consumer deciding between two products, a firm setting its prices, a landlord and tenant negotiating a lease. It examines how supply and demand interact in specific markets, how prices emerge, and how incentives shape behavior. If you want to understand why concert tickets for popular artists tend to sell out instantly, or why rent in certain cities has risen so dramatically, you are asking microeconomic questions.

**Macroeconomics** zooms out to the level of entire national or global economies. It examines aggregate phenomena: total economic output, national unemployment rates, the overall price level, and the money supply. Macroeconomists study how recessions begin and end, what causes inflation to accelerate, and how government spending or tax policy affects economic growth. The 2008 global financial crisis, the inflation surge of 2021-2023, and the economic impact of the COVID-19 pandemic are all macroeconomic events.

## Supply and Demand

The most fundamental concept in economics is the relationship between supply and demand — the mechanism through which market prices are determined in a free economy.

**Demand** refers to how much of a good or service consumers are willing and able to buy at various prices. Generally, as price rises, demand falls (and vice versa): this inverse relationship is the law of demand. **Supply** refers to how much producers are willing to offer at various prices — typically, more at higher prices because higher prices make production more profitable.

**Market equilibrium** is the price at which the quantity supplied exactly matches the quantity demanded. At equilibrium, there is no surplus (excess supply driving prices down) and no shortage (excess demand pushing prices up). When external factors change — a drought reduces the wheat harvest, or a new technology makes solar panels cheaper to produce — the supply or demand curves shift, and a new equilibrium price emerges. Understanding these shifts helps you make sense of price changes you encounter every day, from gasoline to housing to airline tickets.

## Economic Systems

Different societies have organized their economies in fundamentally different ways, each reflecting different beliefs about property, freedom, and fairness.

**Capitalism** is characterized by private ownership of the means of production, market-determined prices, and profit motive as the primary driver of economic activity. It has generated extraordinary wealth and innovation but also produces inequality and can create market failures — situations where unregulated markets produce socially suboptimal outcomes, such as pollution or monopoly.

**Socialism** involves greater public or collective ownership of key industries and resources, with the goal of reducing inequality and ensuring universal access to essential goods. In practice, socialist systems range from Scandinavian-style social democracies (which retain robust private markets alongside generous welfare states) to centrally planned command economies.

**Mixed economies** — the dominant model in most countries today — combine private enterprise with government regulation and public provision of certain goods and services. The United States, Germany, Japan, and Brazil are all mixed economies, differing primarily in the degree and form of state involvement.

## Money and Banking

Money is any widely accepted medium of exchange. It solves the inefficiency of barter (where you must find someone who has what you want and wants what you have) by providing a universally accepted store of value and unit of account. Modern money is primarily **fiat currency** — paper notes and digital entries whose value rests on government decree and public trust rather than on a physical commodity like gold.

**Commercial banks** create money through lending: when a bank issues a loan, it effectively creates new purchasing power. This process is constrained by reserve requirements and capital regulations intended to prevent reckless expansion of credit. **Central banks** — such as the Federal Reserve in the United States, the European Central Bank, and the Bank of England — sit at the apex of each country's financial system. They set benchmark interest rates, manage the money supply, serve as lenders of last resort to commercial banks in crisis, and pursue broad goals like price stability and full employment through **monetary policy**.

## Key Economic Indicators

Economists and policymakers track a set of standard measures to assess the health of an economy:

- **GDP (Gross Domestic Product):** The total market value of all goods and services produced in a country within a given period. Rising GDP generally signals economic expansion; falling GDP over two consecutive quarters defines a recession.
- **Inflation:** The rate at which the general price level rises over time, eroding purchasing power. Central banks typically target around 2% annual inflation as a healthy norm.
- **Unemployment rate:** The percentage of the labor force actively seeking work but unable to find it. Some unemployment is always present ("frictional" unemployment from people between jobs), but high unemployment signals wasted human potential and social distress.
- **Interest rates:** The price of borrowing money. When central banks raise rates, borrowing becomes more expensive, slowing spending and investment; when they lower rates, credit becomes cheaper, stimulating economic activity.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Trade and Globalization">
    International trade allows countries to specialize in producing goods where they have a comparative advantage — meaning they can produce those goods at a lower opportunity cost than other nations. Even if one country is absolutely more efficient at producing everything, both countries can benefit from trade by specializing and exchanging. Globalization has dramatically lowered trade barriers through agreements like NAFTA/USMCA, the WTO framework, and bilateral free trade deals. The result has been an enormous expansion of global wealth, but also disruption for workers in industries exposed to foreign competition and growing concerns about supply chain vulnerability.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Fiscal Policy">
    Fiscal policy refers to government decisions about spending and taxation. During economic downturns, governments may adopt **expansionary fiscal policy** — increasing spending or cutting taxes to inject demand into the economy, a Keynesian approach. During periods of overheating inflation, **contractionary fiscal policy** — reducing spending or raising taxes — helps cool the economy. The trade-off between short-term stimulus and long-term public debt sustainability is one of the most contested debates in economics and politics.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Behavioral Economics">
    Traditional economics assumed that people make rational decisions to maximize their own well-being. Behavioral economics, pioneered by researchers like Daniel Kahneman and Richard Thaler, challenges this assumption by documenting the systematic ways human decision-making deviates from pure rationality. People are loss-averse (feeling losses more acutely than equivalent gains), susceptible to framing effects, prone to present bias (overvaluing immediate rewards), and influenced by default options in powerful ways. These insights have transformed fields from public policy to product design, giving rise to "nudge" strategies that improve outcomes without restricting choice.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
