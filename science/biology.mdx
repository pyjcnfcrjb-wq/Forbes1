Biology is the scientific study of life, which is a fancy way of saying "the study of things that eat, poop, and eventually stop doing both." It ranges from single bacteria — tiny freeloaders that have been thriving since long before you existed — to entire ecosystems, which are essentially very large group chats where everyone is trying to eat everyone else. Biology asks the big questions: What is life? Why does it insist on continuing? And why does every animal, given enough evolutionary time, seem to eventually become a crab?

## The cell: basic unit of life (and gossip)

Every living thing is made of cells, which are basically tiny wet balloons with opinions. Cells come in two flavors: **prokaryotic** (bacteria and archaea — minimalist studio apartments, no nucleus, roommate is your DNA on the floor) and **eukaryotic** (plants, animals, fungi, protists — spacious lofts with a proper nucleus and lots of organelles cluttering the place up).

Key organelles include the **mitochondria**, which generate ATP and, as every biology student learns roughly 400 times, are the "powerhouse of the cell"; the **endoplasmic reticulum**, a membranous factory floor churning out proteins and lipids; the **Golgi apparatus**, which processes, packages, and ships proteins like a very small, very organized post office; and **ribosomes**, tiny bead-like contraptions that assemble proteins one amino acid at a time with the patience of a saint. Plant cells also have **chloroplasts** (solar panels) and a **cell wall** (a rigid green corset). Together, these bits and bobs run the thousands of chemical reactions required to keep you from being a puddle.

## Genetics and heredity

Life's instruction manual is written in **DNA**, a double-stranded molecule twisted like an over-ambitious spiral staircase and stuffed into structures called **chromosomes**. Segments of DNA called **genes** code for proteins, and proteins do basically everything — digest your lunch, contract your muscles, decide whether your earlobes dangle. Humans carry roughly 20,000–25,000 protein-coding genes across 23 pairs of chromosomes, which is fewer than a banana has, and yes, that is a fact people love to bring up at parties.

Enter **Gregor Mendel**, a 19th-century monk who spent his free time cross-breeding pea plants in a monastery garden — arguably the most productive hobby in scientific history. He discovered that traits are passed down as discrete units (genes), and that some are **dominant** (loud) while others are **recessive** (shy). His Laws of Segregation and Independent Assortment still hold up today, which is impressive given that he had no idea what DNA was and probably thought "molecular biology" sounded like a type of pastry.

## Evolution and natural selection

Charles Darwin's theory of **natural selection**, published in _On the Origin of Species_ (1859), is the single biggest idea in biology and also the reason your back hurts. The pitch is disarmingly simple: living things vary, the useful variations tend to stick around, and over many generations you end up with giraffes. Over enough time and enough environmental nagging, populations can diverge into brand-new **species** — a process called **speciation**, and also occasionally "how did we end up with a duck-billed platypus."

Evolution is not a tidy staircase of progress; it's a wildly branching bush where several lineages have independently decided that "crab" is a great look. The fossil record, comparative anatomy, molecular genetics, and directly observable evolution all agree. Antibiotic-resistant bacteria are evolution happening in real time, in real hospitals, at real inconvenience to real doctors — a live demo nobody asked for.

## Ecosystems and ecology

**Ecology** studies how organisms interact with each other and with their surroundings, which is to say: who eats whom, who lives where, and who is quietly ruining it for everyone else. An **ecosystem** is a community of living things plus the non-living stuff (sunlight, water, soil, that one weirdly warm rock). Energy flows through **food chains** and **food webs**: plants catch sunlight, herbivores catch plants, carnivores catch herbivores, and decomposers catch everyone else eventually. Nobody escapes the food web. Nobody.

**Biodiversity** — the variety of species, genes, and ecosystems — is both evolution's greatest hits album and an ecosystem's insurance policy. Diverse ecosystems handle disturbances better; monocultures fall over if you sneeze near them. Earth's major **biomes** — tropical rainforest, temperate forest, grassland, desert, tundra, and ocean — each host their own bizarre cast of characters, from tree frogs the size of a fingernail to fish that glow in the dark for reasons science is still slightly embarrassed about.

## Major groups of living things

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Plants">
    Plants are multicellular, photosynthetic eukaryotes that quietly invented solar power roughly 470 million years before we did. They evolved from green algae, colonized land, and have been standing very still ever since. Modern plants range from mosses and ferns (still emotionally attached to water) to gymnosperms (conifers, the pinecone enthusiasts) and angiosperms (flowering plants, 300,000\+ species and counting, absolutely showing off). Beyond producing the oxygen you're currently using, plants also regulate water cycles, hold soil together, and provide free real estate to a truly alarming number of insects.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Animals">
    Animals are multicellular eukaryotes that decided photosynthesis was too much work and settled on eating other things instead. The animal kingdom is delightfully unhinged: sponges (technically animals, arguably furniture), jellyfish (mostly water with attitude), worms, mollusks, arthropods, echinoderms, and vertebrates — fish, amphibians, reptiles, birds, and mammals. Mammals have hair, warm blood, and a strange compulsion to feed their young with milk. Insects vastly outnumber us, run the pollination economy, and would almost certainly win in a fight if they organized.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Microorganisms">
    Microorganisms — bacteria, archaea, fungi, protists, and viruses — are invisible to the naked eye and yet, statistically speaking, they're winning. Bacteria and archaea live literally everywhere: deep-sea vents, arctic ice, your kitchen sponge, your intestines (thanks for that, by the way). Many are essential decomposers or nitrogen-fixers, so try to be nice about them. Fungi break down dead things and form underground internet cables with plant roots (mycorrhizae — genuinely magical). Viruses may or may not count as "alive" depending on which biologist you ask and how caffeinated they are.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

<Note>
  Biology underpins modern medicine — cells, viruses, and genes are why we have vaccines, cancer therapies, and CRISPR, which is essentially "find and replace" for DNA. Ecology, meanwhile, is why we still have bees, drinkable water, and a functioning climate. Be nice to biology. It's the only reason you exist to read this sentence.
</Note>
