Biology is the scientific study of life in all its forms — from the microscopic machinery of a single bacterial cell to the sweeping interactions of entire ecosystems. As one of the broadest fields in science, biology connects chemistry, physics, and mathematics to answer fundamental questions: What is life? How do organisms grow, reproduce, and respond to their environment? How did today's millions of species come to be? Whether you are examining the double helix of DNA or tracing energy through a food web, biology offers a unified framework for understanding the living world.

## The cell: basic unit of life

Every living thing is made of cells, and understanding cell structure is the foundation of all biological knowledge. Cells fall into two broad categories: **prokaryotic** and **eukaryotic**. Prokaryotic cells — found in bacteria and archaea — lack a membrane-bound nucleus. Their genetic material floats freely in the cytoplasm, and they tend to be smaller and structurally simpler. Eukaryotic cells, which make up plants, animals, fungi, and protists, contain a true nucleus that houses DNA, along with a variety of specialized **organelles**.

Key organelles include the **mitochondria**, which generate ATP through cellular respiration and are often called the "powerhouse of the cell"; the **endoplasmic reticulum**, a network of membranes involved in protein and lipid synthesis; the **Golgi apparatus**, which processes and packages proteins for transport; and **ribosomes**, where proteins are assembled. Plant cells additionally contain **chloroplasts** for photosynthesis and a rigid **cell wall** for structural support. Together, these components carry out the thousands of biochemical reactions that sustain life.

## Genetics and heredity

The instructions for building and operating a living organism are encoded in **deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA)**, a double-stranded molecule wound tightly into structures called **chromosomes**. Segments of DNA called **genes** specify the sequences of amino acids that form proteins, which in turn carry out virtually every function in the cell. Humans carry approximately 20,000–25,000 protein-coding genes distributed across 23 pairs of chromosomes.

Gregor Mendel, a 19th-century monk, laid the groundwork for the study of heredity through his famous pea-plant experiments. He discovered that traits are passed from parents to offspring through discrete units (what we now call genes), and that some traits are **dominant** while others are **recessive**. A dominant allele masks the expression of a recessive allele when both are present. These principles — the Law of Segregation and the Law of Independent Assortment — still underpin modern genetics, even as we now understand the molecular mechanisms behind them through the lens of DNA replication, transcription, and translation.

## Evolution and natural selection

Charles Darwin's theory of **natural selection**, published in *On the Origin of Species* (1859), is arguably the most important unifying concept in biology. The core idea is elegantly simple: individuals within a population vary in their heritable traits; those whose traits better suit them to their environment tend to survive and reproduce more successfully; over many generations, these advantageous traits become more common in the population. Given enough time and environmental pressure, populations can diverge so dramatically that they become separate **species** — a process called **speciation**.

Evolution is not a linear ladder of "progress" but a branching tree of adaptation and diversification. The fossil record, comparative anatomy, molecular genetics, and direct observation all provide overwhelming evidence for evolutionary change. Antibiotic resistance in bacteria is a real-time example: bacterial populations exposed to antibiotics rapidly evolve resistance through natural selection, demonstrating the power and immediacy of the process.

## Ecosystems and ecology

**Ecology** is the study of how organisms interact with one another and with their physical environment. At the heart of ecology is the **ecosystem** — a community of living organisms together with the non-living components of their environment (sunlight, water, soil, temperature). Energy flows through ecosystems via **food chains** and **food webs**: producers (usually plants) capture solar energy through photosynthesis; primary consumers (herbivores) eat producers; secondary and tertiary consumers (carnivores and omnivores) feed further up the chain.

**Biodiversity** — the variety of species, genes, and ecosystems on Earth — is both a product of evolution and a cornerstone of ecosystem health. Rich biodiversity increases resilience: a diverse ecosystem can better absorb disturbances like drought or disease. Earth's major **biomes** — tropical rainforest, temperate deciduous forest, grassland, desert, tundra, and ocean — each harbor distinct communities shaped by climate, soil, and evolutionary history.

## Major groups of living things

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Plants">
    Plants are multicellular, photosynthetic eukaryotes that form the base of most terrestrial food webs. They evolved from green algae and colonized land roughly 470 million years ago. Modern plants range from mosses and ferns — which require moisture to reproduce — to gymnosperms (conifers) and angiosperms (flowering plants), which use seeds for reproduction. Angiosperms are the most diverse plant group, with over 300,000 known species. Plants not only produce oxygen through photosynthesis but also regulate water cycles, stabilize soils, and provide habitat and food for countless other organisms.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Animals">
    Animals are multicellular eukaryotes that obtain energy by consuming other organisms. The animal kingdom is extraordinarily diverse, encompassing invertebrates — sponges, jellyfish, worms, mollusks, arthropods, and echinoderms — as well as vertebrates (fish, amphibians, reptiles, birds, and mammals). Animals exhibit a remarkable range of body plans, reproductive strategies, and behavioral adaptations. Mammals, including humans, are characterized by hair, warm-bloodedness, and the nursing of young with milk. Insects, the most species-rich group of animals, play critical roles as pollinators, decomposers, and prey for countless species.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Microorganisms">
    Microorganisms — including bacteria, archaea, fungi, protists, and viruses — are invisible to the naked eye but dominate life on Earth by sheer numbers and metabolic diversity. Bacteria and archaea are prokaryotic and inhabit virtually every environment, from deep-sea hydrothermal vents to the human gut. Many are essential decomposers, recycling nutrients back into ecosystems. Others fix atmospheric nitrogen, making it available to plants. Fungi break down dead organic matter and form symbiotic relationships with plant roots (mycorrhizae). Though viruses are not universally classified as "alive," they profoundly influence the evolution and health of all other life forms.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

<Note>
  Biology sits at the heart of modern medicine — understanding how cells divide, how viruses replicate, and how genes are expressed has led to vaccines, cancer therapies, and gene-editing tools like CRISPR. Equally, ecological biology informs conservation efforts that protect biodiversity and the ecosystem services — clean water, pollination, climate regulation — that all human societies depend upon.
</Note>
