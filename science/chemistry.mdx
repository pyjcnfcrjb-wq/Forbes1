Chemistry occupies a unique position at the center of the natural sciences. It bridges the subatomic world of physics and the complex molecular machinery of biology, providing the language and tools to understand how matter is structured, how it transforms, and how those transformations power everything from the metabolism of a cell to the combustion of a rocket engine. When you cook a meal, take a medication, or use a cleaning product, you are witnessing chemistry in action. A solid foundation in chemistry equips you to understand the material world at a fundamental level — and to appreciate the elegant logic that connects all forms of matter.

## Atoms and the periodic table

All matter is composed of **atoms** — the smallest units of a chemical element that retain the element's properties. An atom consists of a dense central **nucleus** containing positively charged **protons** and uncharged **neutrons**, surrounded by a cloud of negatively charged **electrons**. The number of protons in the nucleus, called the **atomic number**, defines which element an atom is: hydrogen has 1, carbon has 6, gold has 79. Atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons are called **isotopes**.

The **periodic table** organizes all known elements — 118 as of today — by increasing atomic number and arranges them so that elements with similar chemical properties align in vertical columns called **groups** (or families). Horizontal rows are called **periods**, and an element's period indicates how many electron shells it possesses. The table's structure is not arbitrary: it reflects the filling of electron orbitals and predicts reactivity, electronegativity, atomic radius, and other properties with remarkable accuracy. Group 1 elements (the alkali metals) are highly reactive; Group 18 (noble gases) are nearly inert; the transition metals in the center form a rich landscape of catalytic and magnetic behavior.

## Chemical bonds

Atoms rarely exist in isolation; they combine through **chemical bonds** to form molecules and compounds. The three primary bond types each arise from different interactions between electrons.

**Ionic bonds** form when one atom transfers electrons to another, creating oppositely charged ions that attract each other electrostatically. Sodium chloride (table salt) is the classic example: sodium gives up one electron to chlorine, forming Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions held together in a crystalline lattice. Ionic compounds tend to have high melting points and conduct electricity when dissolved in water.

**Covalent bonds** form when two atoms *share* electrons, rather than transferring them. This sharing allows both atoms to achieve a stable electron configuration. Water (H₂O), carbon dioxide (CO₂), and nearly all organic molecules are held together by covalent bonds. Depending on how equally the electrons are shared, covalent bonds can be **polar** (unequal sharing, as in water) or **nonpolar** (equal sharing, as in molecular oxygen, O₂).

**Metallic bonds** occur in metals, where valence electrons are delocalized and flow freely through a lattice of positive metal ions. This "sea of electrons" explains why metals conduct heat and electricity, and why they are malleable and lustrous.

## Chemical reactions

A **chemical reaction** involves the rearrangement of atoms to convert **reactants** into **products**. The law of conservation of mass demands that the number of each type of atom remain the same on both sides of the equation — a process called **balancing**. For example, the combustion of methane is written: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O. Each side contains one carbon atom, four hydrogen atoms, and four oxygen atoms.

Reactions are also characterized by their energy exchange. **Exothermic reactions** release energy (usually as heat or light) to the surroundings — combustion, explosions, and the rusting of iron are all exothermic. **Endothermic reactions** absorb energy from the surroundings, causing a temperature drop; dissolving ammonium nitrate in water (the chemistry behind instant cold packs) is a familiar example. The rate at which reactions proceed is influenced by temperature, concentration, surface area, and the presence of **catalysts** — substances that lower the activation energy needed to start a reaction without being consumed themselves.

## Chemistry in everyday life

Chemistry is not confined to the laboratory. It permeates daily life in ways that are easy to overlook but impossible to avoid.

**Cooking** is applied chemistry: heat denatures proteins (cooking an egg), the Maillard reaction between amino acids and sugars produces the complex flavors and browning of seared meat, and baking soda (sodium bicarbonate) reacts with acidic ingredients to release CO₂ gas, making baked goods rise.

**Cleaning products** rely on surfactants — molecules with a water-attracting head and an oil-attracting tail — to lift grease off surfaces and suspend it in water for rinsing away. Bleach (sodium hypochlorite) disinfects by releasing reactive chlorine that destroys microbial proteins and DNA.

**Medicines** are precisely engineered molecules designed to interact with specific biological targets. Aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid) inhibits enzymes involved in the production of inflammatory compounds; antibiotics disrupt bacterial cell wall synthesis or protein production without harming human cells.

**Materials** from nylon to Kevlar to silicone were all designed using chemical principles, with properties tuned at the molecular level for specific applications.

## Key chemistry concepts

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Acids and Bases">
    Acids are substances that donate protons (H⁺ ions) in solution; bases accept them. The **pH scale** (0–14) measures acidity and alkalinity: pH 7 is neutral (pure water), values below 7 are acidic, and values above 7 are basic (alkaline). Strong acids like hydrochloric acid (HCl) dissociate completely in water, while weak acids like acetic acid (vinegar) only partially dissociate. Buffer systems — mixtures of weak acids and their conjugate bases — resist pH changes and are critical in biological fluids like blood, which must remain near pH 7.4 to sustain life.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Organic Chemistry">
    Organic chemistry is the study of carbon-containing compounds — a field so vast it constitutes its own discipline. Carbon's ability to form four stable covalent bonds, including chains and rings of carbon atoms, gives rise to an enormous variety of molecular architectures. Organic chemistry encompasses hydrocarbons (fuels), carbohydrates, proteins, lipids, and nucleic acids, as well as synthetic polymers like plastics and fibers. Functional groups — specific clusters of atoms like hydroxyl (–OH), carbonyl (C=O), and amine (–NH₂) — largely determine a molecule's reactivity and biological activity.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="States of Matter">
    Matter commonly exists in three states — **solid**, **liquid**, and **gas** — determined by the balance between the kinetic energy of particles and the intermolecular forces holding them together. In solids, particles vibrate in fixed positions; in liquids, they move freely but remain in close contact; in gases, they move rapidly and independently. A fourth state, **plasma**, consists of ionized gas and is the most abundant form of visible matter in the universe (stars are made of plasma). Phase transitions — melting, freezing, evaporation, condensation, sublimation — involve the absorption or release of energy at constant temperature.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

<Info>
  Laboratory safety is non-negotiable in chemistry. Many chemicals are corrosive, flammable, toxic, or reactive in dangerous ways. Always wear appropriate personal protective equipment (PPE) — including safety goggles, gloves, and a lab coat — work in a well-ventilated space or fume hood, and familiarize yourself with the Safety Data Sheet (SDS) for any substance before use. Proper disposal of chemical waste protects both people and the environment.
</Info>
