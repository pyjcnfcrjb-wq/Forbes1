Earth science — sometimes called geoscience — is an umbrella term for all fields that study planet Earth: its physical structure, the processes that shape its surface, the oceans that cover 71% of it, and the atmosphere that envelops it. It is inherently interdisciplinary, drawing on geology, physics, chemistry, biology, and mathematics to understand a planet that has been continuously changing for 4.5 billion years. From the iron-rich core that generates Earth's protective magnetic field to the jet streams that steer storms across continents, Earth science explains both the slow, vast cycles that operate over geological time and the fast, dramatic events — earthquakes, hurricanes, volcanic eruptions — that reshape the world in hours or minutes.

## Earth's Structure

If you could slice Earth in half like a hard-boiled egg, you would see four distinct layers, each defined by its composition and physical properties.

The **crust** is the thin, rocky outermost shell on which all life exists. Oceanic crust, made primarily of basalt, is dense and typically 5–10 km thick. Continental crust, composed largely of granite and other silica-rich rocks, is thicker (up to 70 km beneath mountain ranges) but less dense. Together, these form the lithosphere — the rigid outer layer that includes the crust and the uppermost mantle.

The **mantle** extends from the base of the crust to a depth of about 2,900 km and makes up roughly 84% of Earth's volume. Although solid, the mantle rock flows extremely slowly over geological time, driven by heat from Earth's interior. This slow, plastic flow is what drives the movement of tectonic plates above.

The **outer core** (2,900–5,100 km depth) is a layer of liquid iron and nickel. Convection currents within this molten metal, combined with Earth's rotation, generate the **geomagnetic field** that shields the planet's surface from harmful solar radiation.

The **inner core** (from 5,100 km to Earth's center at ~6,370 km) is a solid ball of iron and nickel, kept solid by the enormous pressure despite temperatures estimated at 5,000–6,000°C — comparable to the surface of the sun.

## Plate Tectonics

The theory of **plate tectonics**, established in the 1960s, is the unifying framework of geology. Earth's lithosphere is divided into roughly 15 major and several minor **tectonic plates** that drift across the underlying asthenosphere at speeds of 2–15 centimeters per year. The movement of these plates drives the most dramatic geological phenomena on Earth.

At **convergent boundaries**, plates collide. When an oceanic plate meets a continental plate, the denser oceanic plate is forced beneath the other in a process called **subduction**, creating deep ocean trenches and volcanic mountain chains (the Andes are a product of this). When two continental plates collide, neither subducts easily, and the crust crumples upward to form mountain ranges like the Himalayas. At **divergent boundaries**, plates move apart, allowing magma to well up and form new crust — the Mid-Atlantic Ridge is an underwater mountain range created this way, spreading roughly 2.5 cm per year. At **transform boundaries**, plates slide horizontally past each other; the San Andreas Fault in California is a famous example and a significant earthquake source.

**Volcanoes** form both at subduction zones (where water released from the descending plate lowers the melting point of mantle rock) and at hot spots — plumes of unusually hot mantle material rising independently of plate boundaries, as in Hawaii.

## The Atmosphere and Weather

Earth's atmosphere is a thin but critical layer of gases — 78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen, and trace amounts of argon, carbon dioxide, and others — divided into five layers. The **troposphere** (0–12 km) is where virtually all weather occurs. The **stratosphere** (12–50 km) contains the **ozone layer**, which absorbs most of the sun's harmful ultraviolet radiation. The **mesosphere**, **thermosphere**, and **exosphere** extend outward to the boundary with space.

**Weather** describes short-term atmospheric conditions — temperature, humidity, wind, and precipitation — at a specific place and time. It is driven by the uneven heating of Earth's surface by the sun. Warm air rises (creating low-pressure systems) and cool air sinks (creating high-pressure systems); these pressure differences drive winds. Where warm, moist air rises rapidly, thunderstorms develop. Large-scale rotations of air around low-pressure centers form **cyclones** (known as hurricanes or typhoons over warm ocean water), while polar air masses clashing with warmer air masses create the fronts that produce mid-latitude storms.

## Oceans and the Water Cycle

Oceans are not passive bodies of water; they are dynamic systems that regulate Earth's climate, support extraordinary biodiversity, and cycle water, carbon, and nutrients across the planet. **Ocean currents** are driven by wind (surface currents) and by differences in water density caused by temperature and salinity (**thermohaline circulation**). The Gulf Stream, for instance, carries warm tropical water northward along the eastern coast of North America and across to Europe, moderating the climate of countries like the United Kingdom.

The **water cycle** (or hydrological cycle) describes the continuous movement of water through Earth's systems. Solar energy evaporates water from ocean surfaces; this water vapor rises, cools, and condenses around tiny particles to form clouds; precipitation falls as rain or snow; water flows over land in rivers or soaks into the ground to become **groundwater**; and eventually it returns to the ocean. This cycle is the engine of weather, the distributor of freshwater, and a major agent of erosion and landscape formation.

## Climate and Climate Change

**Climate** describes the long-term patterns of temperature, precipitation, and other atmospheric conditions in a region — the average conditions over decades, rather than day-to-day weather. Earth's climate is regulated by many interacting factors: solar output, atmospheric composition, ocean circulation, land surface reflectivity, and volcanic activity.

The **greenhouse effect** is a natural and essential process: gases like water vapor, carbon dioxide (CO₂), and methane trap infrared radiation emitted by Earth's surface, keeping the planet warm enough to support life. Without it, the average surface temperature would be around −18°C rather than the current +15°C. However, since the Industrial Revolution, human activities — especially the burning of fossil fuels and deforestation — have increased atmospheric CO₂ concentrations from approximately 280 parts per million (ppm) to over 420 ppm today, intensifying the greenhouse effect and driving **global warming**. The consequences include rising sea levels from melting ice sheets and glaciers, more frequent and intense extreme weather events, ocean acidification, and shifts in the geographic ranges of species.

<Warning>
  The scientific consensus on human-caused climate change is unequivocal: the past decade has been the warmest on record, and current trajectories point toward significant disruption of food systems, freshwater supplies, coastal infrastructure, and biodiversity. The window for limiting the most severe impacts requires deep, rapid reductions in greenhouse gas emissions. Understanding the science of climate change is the first step toward informed action.
</Warning>

<Note>
  Earth science offers a wide range of rewarding careers. Geologists work in natural resource extraction, hazard assessment, and environmental remediation. Meteorologists forecast weather and study atmospheric dynamics. Oceanographers investigate marine ecosystems and ocean-climate interactions. Seismologists monitor earthquakes and help engineer safer buildings. Climate scientists model the future of Earth's climate to inform policy. If you are drawn to understanding the planet beneath your feet, the skies overhead, or the oceans that connect continents, Earth science offers a career path with both intellectual depth and real-world impact.
</Note>
