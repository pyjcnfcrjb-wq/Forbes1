Nutrition is the foundation of your physical health, energy levels, and long-term well-being. Every cell in your body depends on the nutrients you consume — from the proteins that rebuild muscle tissue to the vitamins that keep your immune system functioning. Understanding nutrition doesn't mean following a rigid diet plan; it means developing a working knowledge of what food does inside your body so you can make informed, sustainable choices every day.

## Macronutrients

Macronutrients are the three major categories of nutrients your body needs in large quantities to produce energy and support growth, repair, and vital functions. Each one plays a distinct and irreplaceable role.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Carbohydrates">
    Carbohydrates are your body's preferred energy source. When you eat carbs, your digestive system breaks them down into glucose, which fuels your brain, muscles, and organs. There are two main types: **simple carbohydrates** (sugars that digest quickly, like fruit and table sugar) and **complex carbohydrates** (starches and fiber that digest slowly, providing sustained energy).

    Fiber — a form of carbohydrate your body can't fully digest — is especially important for gut health, blood sugar regulation, and reducing the risk of cardiovascular disease.

    **Good sources:**
    - Whole grains (oats, brown rice, quinoa, whole wheat bread)
    - Legumes (lentils, chickpeas, black beans)
    - Vegetables (sweet potatoes, corn, peas)
    - Fruits (bananas, apples, berries)
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Proteins">
    Proteins are the structural building blocks of your body. They're made up of amino acids, nine of which are considered "essential" because your body can't synthesize them on its own — you must obtain them from food. Protein supports muscle repair and growth, enzyme production, immune function, and the transport of molecules through your bloodstream.

    The recommended dietary allowance (RDA) for protein is approximately 0.8 grams per kilogram of body weight for sedentary adults, but active individuals and older adults often benefit from higher intakes.

    **Good sources:**
    - Animal proteins: chicken, turkey, eggs, fish, lean beef, dairy
    - Plant proteins: tofu, tempeh, edamame, lentils, beans, seitan
    - Nuts and seeds (almonds, pumpkin seeds, hemp seeds)
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Fats">
    Dietary fat is essential — not something to be feared. Fats support brain health (your brain is roughly 60% fat), hormone production, fat-soluble vitamin absorption (A, D, E, K), and cell membrane integrity. The key is distinguishing between types of fat.

    **Unsaturated fats** (monounsaturated and polyunsaturated, including omega-3 and omega-6 fatty acids) are associated with reduced inflammation and improved heart health. **Saturated fats**, found largely in animal products, are fine in moderation. **Trans fats**, found in many processed and fried foods, are strongly linked to cardiovascular disease and should be minimized.

    **Good sources:**
    - Avocados and olive oil
    - Fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines)
    - Nuts and nut butters
    - Flaxseeds and chia seeds
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Micronutrients

While macronutrients provide energy, micronutrients — vitamins and minerals — regulate the biochemical processes that keep you alive and healthy. You need them in smaller amounts, but deficiencies can have serious consequences.

**Key vitamins** include:
- **Vitamin D**: Supports bone health and immune function; many people are deficient, especially in low-sunlight regions.
- **Vitamin B12**: Essential for nerve function and red blood cell production; found almost exclusively in animal products, making supplementation important for vegans.
- **Vitamin C**: A powerful antioxidant that supports immune defense and collagen synthesis.
- **Folate (B9)**: Critical for DNA synthesis; especially important during pregnancy to prevent neural tube defects.

**Key minerals** include:
- **Iron**: Carries oxygen in your blood; deficiency causes fatigue and anemia. Heme iron from meat is more readily absorbed than non-heme iron from plants.
- **Calcium**: The primary mineral in your bones and teeth; also involved in muscle contraction and nerve signaling.
- **Magnesium**: Involved in over 300 enzymatic reactions, including energy production and blood pressure regulation.
- **Zinc**: Supports immune function, wound healing, and protein synthesis.

Eating a wide variety of colorful whole foods is the most reliable way to meet your micronutrient needs without supplementation.

## Reading Food Labels

Food labels are powerful tools for making informed choices, but they can be confusing without a framework for interpretation.

- **Serving size**: Always start here. All values on the label are based on a single serving, which may be much smaller than what you actually eat in one sitting.
- **Calories**: Reflects total energy per serving. This number alone doesn't tell you about food quality.
- **% Daily Value (%DV)**: Based on a 2,000-calorie reference diet, these percentages show how much a nutrient one serving contributes. Generally, 5% DV or less is considered low; 20% DV or more is considered high.
- **Macronutrient breakdown**: Look at total fat, carbohydrates (including fiber and sugars), and protein.
- **Added sugars**: Distinct from naturally occurring sugars (like those in fruit or dairy), added sugars contribute calories with little nutritional value. The American Heart Association recommends limiting added sugars to 25g per day for women and 36g for men.
- **Ingredients list**: Ingredients are listed in descending order by weight. If refined sugar, hydrogenated oils, or unrecognizable chemicals appear near the top, the product is likely highly processed.

## Building a Balanced Diet

No single food provides everything your body needs. A balanced diet draws from a variety of food groups in appropriate proportions.

The **plate model** is a practical visual guide: aim to fill half your plate with vegetables and fruits, a quarter with whole grains or complex carbohydrates, and a quarter with a lean protein source. Add a small amount of healthy fat and a serving of dairy or dairy alternative.

**Variety** matters as much as proportion. Different colored vegetables contain different phytonutrients and antioxidants — eating the rainbow isn't just catchy advice, it's evidence-based. **Moderation** means no food is inherently forbidden; ultra-processed foods, sweets, and alcohol can be part of a healthy life when consumed occasionally and mindfully.

**Hydration** is often overlooked but is nutritionally critical. Water supports digestion, nutrient transport, temperature regulation, and kidney function. Most adults need roughly 2–3 liters of fluid per day, though individual needs vary by activity level, climate, and body size.

<Warning>
  Be cautious of fad diets that promise rapid results by eliminating entire food groups or relying on unproven supplements. Many are nutritionally incomplete, unsustainable, and sometimes dangerous. The diet industry is rife with misinformation — claims that sound scientific often are not peer-reviewed or are based on poorly designed studies. Sustainable, balanced eating outperforms extreme diets over the long term.
</Warning>

<Tip>
  If you have specific health goals, medical conditions, or complex dietary needs, consider consulting a **registered dietitian (RD)**. Unlike general nutritionists (a less regulated title in many countries), registered dietitians undergo rigorous academic training and clinical practice hours. They can provide personalized guidance rooted in current evidence.
</Tip>
